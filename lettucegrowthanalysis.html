The Growth of Lettuce: An Environmental Data Analysis

Objective:

The main goal of the project is to answer the following key questions:

  1. What’s the correlation between TDS and lettuce growth during germination?
  1. What are the optimal conditions for successful lettuce growth?

Methodology:

  • Data Source:

The dataset used in this project was downloaded from Kaggle and contains daily measurements on Plant ID, temperature, pH, TDS, and growth rate over several stages of lettuce development.

The Excel file is available in a view-only format to preserve the charts and data. For those who would like to interact with the data and conduct their own analysis, a downloadable version is also available.

Data Cleaning:

I removed unnecessary columns (duplicate humidity and Fahrenheit values) and ensured there were no missing or outlier values.

  • Phases of Growth:

    I segmented the growth stages as follows:

    1. Seedling Stage: Days 0–6
    1. Germination Stage 1: Days 7–15
    1. Germination Stage 2: Days 16–30
    1. Mature Stage: Days 40–70.


Analysis & Key Findings:

  • Correlation between TDS and Growth:

    Scatter plots showed that increased TDS levels did not have a positive impact on the growth rate, particularly during the germination stage.

  • Optimal Growth Conditions:

    Analysis revealed that a combination of moderate temperature, slightly acidic pH, and balanced TDS levels facilitated the most rapid growth during the early phases.

  • Growth Rate Observations:

    Growth rate consistency was observed for most plant IDs across different stages, except for ID 1, which showed increased growth in the mature stage.

    Conclusion:

    From this scatter plot, it is evident that TDS is not a significant driver of lettuce growth. While TDS is present in the soil, the data suggests that its influence on growth is minimal. Growth can occur with or without TDS, and in many instances, TDS does not correspond to any observable growth. This indicates that while TDS may be necessary in small amounts, it is not the primary factor that influences lettuce growth in this dataset.

    The polynomial trend line further highlights this by showing that while TDS and growth run parallel, they are not directly influencing each other. TDS might be playing a background role, but other factors (such as temperature, pH, or other nutrients) may have a stronger impact on growth.

    Therefore, the presence of TDS in the soil may be beneficial, but it is not a critical factor in determining the success of lettuce growth based on this analysis.
