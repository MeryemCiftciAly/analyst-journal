Claims Volume Analysis Using SQL

How would you identify trends in claim volumes over time to assist in resource allocation?

Step 1: Initial Query and Outline

I first identified the necessary columns for the analysis and outlined the structure of my query. Assuming the table has columns for year and month derived from the claim date (in the format yyyy-mm-dd), my initial query looks as follows: 

            SELECT YEAR(claim_date) AS "Claim Year", 
                   MONTH(claim_date) AS "Claim Month", 
                   COUNT(claim_id) AS "Total Claims" 
            FROM claims 
            GROUP BY YEAR(claim_date), MONTH(claim_date) 
            ORDER BY claim_year, claim_month;

Step 2: Narrowing the Focus

I refined the focus to a specific year and month, assuming there’s a year and month column for claim dates. I used claim_id to count unique claims for each period. The result is a table with columns for year, month, and total_claims. 

            SELECT YEAR(claim_date) AS "Claim Year", 
                   MONTHNAME(claim_date) AS "Claim Month", 
                   COUNT(claim_id) AS "Total Claims" 
            FROM claims 
            WHERE YEAR(claim_date) BETWEEN 2011 AND 2013 
            GROUP BY YEAR(claim_date), MONTH(claim_date) 
            ORDER BY claim_year, claim_month;

Visualization

The results can be visualized using a line chart, which will show trends in claims over time. For example, a line chart could display data across a two-year span or show claims per month, allowing us to spot patterns and identify periods with higher or lower claim volumes.

Possible Output

The analysis will allow us to identify trends in claim volumes and optimize resource allocation to better handle peak times.

Further Refinement: Analyzing Claim Types and Recent Claims

Possible Output: Claim Volumes and Claim Types

            2011        January      12

Step 3: Refining the Focus to Include Claim Types

To gain a deeper understanding, we can refine the query to also categorize the claims by their type, such as 'Flood.' The updated query would look like this: 

            SELECT 
                YEAR(claim_date) AS "Claim_year", 
                MONTHNAME(claim_date) AS "Claim_month", 
                COUNT(claim_id) AS "Total_claims", 
                claim_type AS "Claim_type" 
            FROM claims 
            WHERE YEAR(claim_date) BETWEEN 2011 AND 2013 
                AND claim_type = 'Flood' 
            GROUP BY YEAR(claim_date), MONTH(claim_date), claim_type 
            ORDER BY claim_year, claim_month;

This query will allow us to see the volume of 'Flood' claims over the given period, and the possible output could be: 

            Claim_year  Claim_month  Total_claims  Claim_type
            2011        January      8             Flood
            2012        March        10            Flood
            2013        December     5             Flood

Identifying the Most Recent Claims

To further refine our analysis and focus on the most recent claims, we can modify the query to capture the range between the earliest and most recent claim dates. The query would be as follows: 

            SELECT * 
            FROM claims 
            WHERE claim_date BETWEEN 
                (SELECT MIN(claim_date) FROM claims) 
                AND 
                (SELECT MAX(claim_date) FROM claims);

This query will give us the claims within the date range from the earliest to the most recent date. A sample output would look like this: 

            claim_id  claim_date
            1         2010-01-15
            2         2015-03-22
            3         2018-07-10
            4         2020-09-05

Real-World Application

This analysis is invaluable for understanding claim patterns and making informed decisions on resource allocation. For example, an insurance company could use this data to allocate resources effectively, such as budgeting for overtime during periods of high claim volumes (e.g., flood events).

Fraud Detection - Identifying Potentially Fraudulent Claims

Scenario Overview

In this scenario, I focus on identifying potentially fraudulent claims based on the claim amounts exceeding a predefined threshold. The goal is to spot claims that may be unusually high, flagging them for further investigation.

Approach

I began the analysis by identifying the relevant columns needed for the investigation, including the claim amount and claimant information. The next step was to refine the query based on company policies or predefined guidelines to flag claims that could indicate potential fraud.

Step 1: Mapping Out the Necessary Columns

First, I mapped out the columns I would need from the dataset to gather the basic information required for fraud detection. This involved reviewing the dataset to identify key fields such as claim_id, claimant_name, and claim_amount, as well as any other relevant details like claim_type.

Step 2: Defining the Threshold for Fraud Detection

With an understanding of the average claim and the necessary columns in hand, I proceeded to enhance the query by applying predefined company policies, which specify thresholds for identifying high-risk claims. Claims that exceed a certain amount, such as $10,000, are flagged for further investigation.

Sample SQL Query

Here’s the query used to identify potentially fraudulent claims based on the claim amount exceeding a threshold: 

SELECT claim_id, claimant_name, claim_amount,
       CASE
           WHEN claim_amount > 10000 THEN 'High Claim'
           WHEN claim_amount BETWEEN 5000 AND 10000 THEN 'Moderate Risk'
           ELSE 'Normal'
       END AS Flag
FROM claims
WHERE claim_amount > 5000
ORDER BY claim_amount DESC;

Possible Output

The output of the query flags claims based on the claim amount, with each claim being classified as 'High Claim', 'Moderate Risk', or 'Normal'. Here’s an example output:

Claim ID Claimant Name Claim Amount Flag
101 John Doe 12,000 High Claim
102 Jane Smith 7,500 Moderate Risk
103 Alice Brown 3,000 Normal
104 Bob Johnson 15,000 High Claim
105 Emma Davis 9,000 Moderate Risk

Further Investigation

For instance, Emma Davis's claim amount of $9,000 falls under the 'Moderate Risk' category. Even though the claim amount is below $10,000, further investigation may be necessary if this amount is significantly higher than her historical claims.
To confirm if this is a sudden spike, an additional query could be executed to examine Emma Davis’s previous claims history. If her previous claims have been consistently low, this could indicate potential fraud and would warrant further review before a settlement.

Example Query for Historical Claims Analysis

To check for historical claims trends, the query could look like this: 

SELECT claim_id, claimant_name, claim_amount
FROM claims
WHERE claimant_name = 'Emma Davis'
ORDER BY claim_date;
